  title: "The Challenge"
  content: |
    Public safety is the foundation of a healthy community. When people feel safe in their neighborhoods, their homes, and on their streets, everything else becomes possible. Education improves. Businesses thrive. Families invest in their futures.

    Today, Davis County faces a critical law enforcement crisis. Police departments across the region are struggling with officer recruitment and retention. Many experienced officers are leaving for better pay and working conditions. New recruits struggle with mental health challenges and PTSD at alarming rates. Meanwhile, 911 response times are increasing and officer morale is declining.

    This isn't a problem we can ignore. It's a crisis that demands real solutions—better pay, mental health support, equipment investment, and community partnership. Kara believes public safety requires supporting those who serve while also addressing the root causes of crime.

sections:
  - eyebrow: "Supporting Officers"
    title: "Recruitment, Retention, and Compensation"
    content: |
      We can't have public safety without police officers. And we can't have officers without competitive compensation and working conditions. Kara supports:

      - County salary supplements to make law enforcement competitive with neighboring agencies
      - Sign-on bonuses for experienced officers moving to Davis County
      - Tuition reimbursement and educational development programs
      - Equipment investment to keep officers safe and effective
      - Community liaison roles that reduce officer stress and build trust
      - Career advancement pathways that retain experienced leaders

      Police officers choose where to work. We need to make Davis County the place they want to serve.

  - eyebrow: "Mental Health Crisis"
    title: "Supporting First Responder Mental Health"
    content: |
      Thirty percent of law enforcement officers struggle with PTSD and other mental health challenges. First responders see things that trauma our minds. Peer suicide rates among officers are multiple times the general population. This is a crisis within our public safety system.

      Kara's approach:
      - Comprehensive mental health services specifically designed for law enforcement
      - Peer support programs staffed by officers who understand the job
      - Mandatory mental health check-ins and destigmatization
      - Crisis intervention teams to support officers in distress
      - Family support services to help spouses and children deal with officer trauma
      - Regular training on recognizing and addressing mental health issues

      Supporting officer mental health isn't a luxury—it's essential to maintaining effective, safe communities.

  - eyebrow: "Community Policing"
    title: "Building Trust and Partnership"
    content: |
      Public safety works best when law enforcement and communities work together. Police who know and are trusted by their neighborhoods are more effective. Residents who trust police are more likely to report crimes and cooperate. Kara supports:

      - Community liaison officers in neighborhoods
      - Regular community meetings and problem-solving partnerships
      - Training in de-escalation and implicit bias
      - Accountability measures that maintain community trust
      - Youth engagement programs that build relationships early
      - Transparent reporting on police activities and outcomes

      Great policing requires great relationships between officers and communities.

  - eyebrow: "Root Causes"
    title: "Addressing Drug Addiction and Prevention"
    content: |
      Crime often stems from addiction, mental health challenges, and lack of opportunity. Effective public safety addresses these root causes:

      - Treatment programs for addiction that work alongside enforcement
      - Youth prevention programs focused on education and opportunity
      - Mental health services for people in crisis
      - Coordination between law enforcement, courts, and social services
      - Support for reentry programs that reduce recidivism
      - Community economic development that creates opportunity

      Enforcement is necessary, but prevention and treatment are more effective long-term.
